Sustainable Fashion: How to Actually Shop Better

Updated July 2026 · 7 min read

"Sustainable" is the most overused word in fashion, and one of the least defined. Almost every brand now has an eco-collection, a green tag, a page about their values. Some of it is real. A lot of it is marketing.

This is a practical guide to telling the difference — what the term actually means, how to spot a genuine claim from a greenwashed one, and how to build a wardrobe that's kinder to the planet without needing to trust anyone's advertising.

What "sustainable" actually covers

It isn't one thing. A garment touches the environment at several points, and a brand can be good at one and terrible at another:

  • Materials — what it's made of. Organic cotton, linen, hemp, and recycled fibres have a lighter footprint than virgin polyester or conventional cotton.
  • Production — how and where it's made, how much water and energy it takes, and how workers are treated. "Eco" fabric sewn in an exploitative factory isn't sustainable in any meaningful sense.
  • Longevity — how long it lasts. This is the one everyone forgets, and it's arguably the most important. A well-made garment worn for years beats a "sustainable" one that falls apart in a season.
  • End of life — whether it can be recycled, resold, or composted, or whether it goes to landfill.
The most sustainable garment is the one you already own — and after that, the one you'll still wear in five years.

How to spot greenwashing

Greenwashing is when a brand spends more effort *sounding* sustainable than *being* it. A few reliable tells:

Vague words with nothing behind them. "Eco-friendly," "conscious," "natural," "green" — these have no legal definition and mean whatever the brand wants. Real claims are specific: "made with 60% recycled polyester," "certified organic cotton," "GOTS certified."

  • A tiny "conscious" line beside a giant fast-fashion business. If one collection is green and the other ten thousand products aren't, the green line is a marketing hedge, not a mission.
  • Certifications you can check vs. logos they invented. Genuine third-party standards — GOTS, OEKO-TEX, Fair Trade, B Corp, GRS — can be verified. A brand's own "eco" badge means nothing.
  • Nature imagery doing the heavy lifting. Green leaves, earth tones and photos of forests on the packaging tell you about the art director, not the supply chain.
  • No transparency. Brands doing real work tend to publish it — where things are made, what the materials are, sometimes the factory list. Silence on specifics is a flag.

The certifications actually worth knowing

You don't need to memorise these, but recognising a few genuine standards cuts through most of the noise:

  • GOTS (Global Organic Textile Standard) — organic fibres, plus environmental and social criteria through the whole chain.
  • OEKO-TEX — tested for harmful substances.
  • GRS (Global Recycled Standard) — verifies recycled content claims.
  • Fair Trade / Fair Wear — focused on the people making the clothes.
  • B Corp — a company-wide social and environmental standard, not garment-specific but a strong signal.

How to actually shop better

Here's the part that doesn't depend on trusting a single brand's claims — because it's about how you buy, not what you're sold.

Buy less, choose well

The single most impactful thing, and the least glamorous. A smaller wardrobe of pieces you genuinely wear is more sustainable than any eco-collection. Our guide to the seven bags you actually need and the transitional dressing approach are both built on this idea: fewer things, chosen to combine.

Prioritise longevity over labels

A well-constructed garment in a durable natural fibre will outlast three "sustainable" fast-fashion pieces — and outlasting is the whole point. Learn to read quality: the way a shoe is built, the weight of a fabric, the finish of a seam.

Consider pre-owned and resale

Nothing is greener than a garment that already exists. The resale market for quality and designer pieces has never been bigger, and buying second-hand keeps good clothing in circulation and out of landfill.

The one-question test before you buy: "Will I still wear this in three years?" If yes, buy the best version you can. If no, the material on the label barely matters — it's destined to be waste either way.

Care for what you own

Wash less and cooler, air-dry, repair rather than replace, and store things properly. Extending a garment's life by even a year meaningfully cuts its footprint — and it's free.
