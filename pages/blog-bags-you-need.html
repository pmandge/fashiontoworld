The Only 7 Bags You Actually Need

Updated July 2026 · 7 min read

Most people own too many bags and still stand in front of the cupboard with nothing to carry. That's not a shopping problem — it's a coverage problem. Seven bags own the vast majority of the situations you'll actually face, and once you have them, everything else is decoration.

Here's the full set: what each one is genuinely for, what to look for when you buy, and the mistake most people make.

1. The everyday tote

The workhorse. If you carry a laptop, a notebook, a water bottle and the general debris of a working day, this is the bag doing 60% of the labour in your week.

Look for: a flat base (so it stands up), a top zip or magnetic closure, and a strap drop long enough to clear a coat shoulder.

The mistake: buying one too big. An oversized tote doesn't hold more — it just lets everything sink to the bottom. If it fits a 13-15" laptop with a hand's width to spare, it's big enough.

2. The crossbody

The one you'll reach for most often and think about least. It exists for a single purpose: keeping your hands free while your belongings stay in front of you, not swinging behind.

Look for: an adjustable strap (non-negotiable), and a body small enough that it sits on your hip, not below it.

The mistake: a strap you can't shorten. A crossbody that hangs too low turns into a swinging liability and defeats the entire point.

3. The structured top-handle

This is your "I need to be taken seriously" bag — interviews, meetings, anywhere the bag speaks before you do. A slouchy hobo says weekend; a structured top-handle says you have your affairs in order.

Look for: a firm base and sides that hold their shape when empty. Structure is the whole proposition — if it collapses, it isn't doing its job.

The mistake: buying it in a novelty colour. This is the one bag where black, tan or navy earns its keep, because it needs to work with everything formal you own.

4. The evening bag

Small, unnecessary, and completely indispensable about six times a year. A clutch or micro-bag exists to hold a phone, a card and a lipstick, and to not look like you brought your day to a dinner.

Look for: a detachable chain — so it works as both a clutch and a shoulder bag, which doubles the occasions it covers.

The mistake: spending too much. This is the lowest-mileage bag you'll own. Buy something that looks expensive rather than something that is.

5. The weekender

Two nights away, and a suitcase is overkill. The weekender is the bag that makes short trips feel effortless instead of like a logistics exercise.

Look for: a trolley sleeve (the strap on the back that slides over a suitcase handle) if you travel often, and a wide opening — you want to see what's in it, not excavate.

The mistake: ignoring the shoulder strap. A weekender carried only by its handles gets very heavy, very quickly, somewhere between the car and the platform.

6. The backpack

The one people resist and then use constantly. For commuting, travel days and anything involving stairs or distance, two straps beat one every single time — and your spine will make the argument for you eventually.

Look for: a leather or structured finish if you want it to read as "considered" rather than "school run". Padded laptop sleeve if it's your commuter.

The mistake: assuming a backpack can't look smart. A clean leather backpack works with tailoring; a nylon sports one does not.

7. The bag you love for no practical reason

Yes, really. Every functional list needs one slot for the bag that doesn't justify itself — the colour you love, the shape that isn't sensible, the one you bought because it made you happy.

A wardrobe of purely rational purchases is a joyless thing. Six bags cover your life; the seventh is the one you'll actually remember buying.

How to buy a bag online without regretting it

  • Check the dimensions, not the photo. Bags are photographed to flatter. A listed height of 18cm is small — hold a ruler up before you commit.
  • Read what the lining is made of. A beautiful exterior with a flimsy lining will fail from the inside out, and you can't repair what you can't reach.
  • Hardware is the tell. Zips and clasps are where cheap bags die. If the listing shows a close-up of the hardware, that's usually a good sign — brands hide what they're not proud of.
  • Buy the classic, not the trend. A well-chosen tan crossbody will outlive four seasons of novelty shapes.
